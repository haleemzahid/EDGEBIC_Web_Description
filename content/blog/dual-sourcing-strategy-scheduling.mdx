The post-COVID era ended single-source complacency. Manufacturers who spent decades squeezing suppliers down to the lowest-cost single source learned an expensive lesson when that source went offline: a few cents per unit in savings is not worth a multi-week production stoppage.

Dual sourcing is the answer. But dual sourcing creates its own complexity — specifically, scheduling across two suppliers with different lead times, different costs, different quality levels, and different capacity availability. Getting the allocation wrong means you either underutilize your secondary supplier (who then declines in capability and engagement) or overutilize them (and pay an unnecessary cost premium).

At User Solutions, we have helped manufacturers navigate dual-sourcing complexity since long before it became an industry mandate. This post gives you the practical framework for when to dual-source, how to allocate, and how to model it in your scheduling and MRP systems.

## When Dual Sourcing Is Worth the Investment

Dual sourcing is not free. It costs management attention, supplier relationship overhead, qualification time and expense, and a unit cost premium on the secondary share. Before committing, assess whether the risk justifies the investment.

Run this risk analysis for each sole-source component:

**1. Criticality**: Is this component on the critical path for production? A shortage stops the line — no substitution is possible. High criticality.

**2. Lead time risk**: What is the procurement lead time? Long lead times (8+ weeks) mean you cannot spot-source in an emergency — by the time you find an alternative, your line has been down for months. High risk.

**3. Supplier concentration**: Does your current supplier have meaningful market alternatives, or are they the only qualified source? Sole-source with no obvious alternatives = extreme risk.

**4. Disruption history**: Has this supplier or component category caused a production disruption in the past 3 years? Historical disruption is a strong predictor of future disruption.

**5. Component value**: High-value components that represent a significant share of COGS warrant more supply chain protection than commodity items.

If a component scores high on 3 or more of these factors, dual sourcing is justified. If it scores high on all 5, dual sourcing is urgent.

A useful shorthand: any sole-source component with a lead time above 6 weeks and criticality for more than 20% of your production volume should have a dual-source project initiated immediately.

## The 70/30 Split: Standard Allocation Logic

The most widely used dual-source allocation is a 70/30 or 80/20 split between primary and secondary suppliers. The logic:

- **Primary supplier (70–80%)**: typically lower cost, higher volume familiarity, shorter lead time, better quality history. Gets the majority of volume.
- **Secondary supplier (20–30%)**: typically higher cost or slightly longer lead time, but qualified and active. Receives enough volume to maintain process familiarity, keep your account as a priority, and remain operationally capable.

The 20–30% floor for the secondary supplier is important. Secondary suppliers who receive only 5–10% of volume often deprioritize your account — you are too small a customer for them to maintain dedicated capacity. When you actually need them in a crisis, they cannot respond quickly. A 25–30% secondary share keeps you meaningful to them.

The split should not be fixed indefinitely. Review it quarterly against supplier performance data:

- If primary supplier OTD falls below 80%, shift 10–15 percentage points to secondary
- If secondary supplier quality PPM exceeds 2,000, freeze secondary share and accelerate qualification of a tertiary source
- If primary supplier has a capacity warning, pre-position orders to secondary before the shortage hits

## Scheduling Across Mismatched Lead Times

The most technically challenging aspect of dual sourcing is scheduling purchase orders correctly when the two suppliers have different lead times. If your primary supplier delivers in 4 weeks and your secondary in 6 weeks, a simple total-quantity calculation will mislead you.

Consider a scenario where you need 1,000 units in week 10. With a 70/30 split:
- Primary order: 700 units, released in week 6 (4-week lead time)
- Secondary order: 300 units, released in week 4 (6-week lead time)

The secondary order must be released 2 weeks earlier than the primary order to arrive at the same time. If your MRP treats both suppliers with the same planning lead time, one of three bad things happens:
1. You release both orders at week 6, and 300 units arrive 2 weeks late
2. You release both orders at week 4, and 700 units arrive 2 weeks early (excess inventory)
3. You only order from the primary in week 6 and scramble for the secondary 300 units when you realize the shortage

The fix is to configure your MRP system with separate supplier records, each carrying the correct planning lead time. Most modern MRP systems support this — the component has one item master, but procurement rules can specify multiple approved vendors with individual lead times, minimum order quantities, and allocation percentages.

In Excel-based scheduling environments (which many SMB manufacturers still use), the practical solution is a dual-source planning worksheet that separately calculates primary and secondary order release dates and tracks open POs by supplier.

## Qualifying Your Secondary Supplier Before You Need Them

The most common mistake in dual-sourcing programs: treating qualification as something you do after a disruption. Qualification takes 3–6 months for complex components. If your primary supplier goes down today and you have not qualified a backup, you have 0 backup — not a 30% backup.

Qualification must happen proactively, during normal operations. The standard sequence:

**Step 1 — Supplier evaluation** (2–4 weeks): Identify candidates, issue RFQ with full engineering drawings and specifications, evaluate quotes on cost, lead time, quality system (ISO 9001 minimum, AS9100 for aerospace, IATF 16949 for automotive), and capacity availability.

**Step 2 — First article inspection** (4–6 weeks): The candidate supplier produces a small sample lot — typically 5–25 pieces for machined components. Your quality team performs dimensional and functional inspection against drawing requirements. For critical components, this step often involves supplier on-site visits.

**Step 3 — Production trial** (4–8 weeks): The supplier produces a production-scale trial run. You inspect the incoming material, measure quality PPM, confirm lead time performance, and evaluate packaging and documentation quality.

**Step 4 — AVL approval** (2–4 weeks): Add the supplier to your approved vendor list (AVL). This typically requires engineering sign-off on the first article results and procurement sign-off on the commercial terms (pricing, payment, lead time commitments).

Total timeline: 12–22 weeks for most custom components. Start the qualification now. Do not wait for the disruption.

## The Cost Premium of Dual Sourcing vs. Single-Source Risk

Manufacturers who resist dual sourcing almost always cite cost: "Our secondary supplier is 12% more expensive. We cannot afford to pay that on 30% of our volume."

Do the math. A 12% premium on 30% of volume is a 3.6% blended cost increase for that component. On a $50 component, that is $1.80 per unit.

Now calculate the expected cost of a single-source disruption. In a recent analysis across five mid-sized manufacturers, the average disruption from a sole-source component failure cost:
- $35,000–$80,000 in direct overtime and expediting costs
- $50,000–$200,000 in customer penalties and expedited shipping
- 2–5% customer retention risk on affected accounts

At a 15% annual disruption probability (conservative for a sole-source component with no alternative), the expected annual disruption cost on a component that touches $2M of production volume is $7,500–$42,000 per year. The cost of the 3.6% dual-source premium on $2M is $72,000. In this case, the premium is not obviously cheaper — but the calculation changes dramatically when you include the customer retention risk. Losing one customer account because of a supply chain disruption they attributed to your single-source dependency can cost far more than years of price premiums.

The right framing is not "is the secondary supplier cheaper than the primary" — it is "is the premium cheaper than the insurance value of the backup."

## Managing Quality Across Two Sources

Dual sourcing introduces a quality complexity that single sourcing avoids: your incoming material may come from either source, and the two sources may have slightly different dimensional means, surface finish characteristics, or process signatures. For many components this is irrelevant. For precision-fit components, it matters.

Three practices manage dual-source quality risk:

**1. Lot segregation and traceability**: Tag each incoming lot with supplier identity and maintain lot-level traceability through your production process. If a quality escape occurs, you can determine whether it is a supplier-specific issue or a process issue.

**2. Acceptance criteria by source**: For components where supplier-specific variation is a known risk, establish separate incoming inspection acceptance criteria for each supplier, reflecting their process capability. A supplier with a demonstrated Cpk of 1.8 on a critical dimension can be accepted at standard spec. A newer supplier with Cpk of 1.2 should face tighter incoming inspection to catch drift before it affects your process.

**3. Periodic process capability audits**: Annually (minimum), measure the process capability of both suppliers on your critical characteristics. A supplier whose Cpk is trending downward is giving you early warning of a future quality problem. Respond by increasing secondary-supplier share while the primary supplier addresses their process.

## Modeling Dual-Source Allocation in MRP

The mechanics of dual-source MRP configuration vary by system, but the logical requirements are consistent:

- Each supplier is a separate procurement record with its own lead time, minimum order quantity, and preferred order multiple
- Allocation percentages are defined at the item-supplier level (70% primary, 30% secondary)
- MRP generates separate planned orders for each supplier based on their allocation share and lead time
- Open POs from both suppliers are visible in material availability calculations, preventing double-counting

If your MRP does not support native dual-source allocation, a workaround is to create two separate item numbers — one for primary-sourced material, one for secondary-sourced material — and manage the allocation manually in your planning process. This is operationally clunky but works if your MRP is single-vendor only.

## Decision Framework: Is Dual Sourcing Right for This Component?

Use this quick framework to triage your component portfolio:

| Factor | Score |
|---|---|
| Component is on critical production path | +30 |
| Procurement lead time > 8 weeks | +20 |
| Currently sole-sourced | +25 |
| Primary supplier OTD < 85% | +20 |
| Component is > 5% of COGS | +15 |
| Prior disruption event in last 3 years | +25 |

**Score 80+**: Dual-source immediately. Start qualification this quarter.
**Score 50–79**: Dual-source within 12 months. Start supplier identification now.
**Score 30–49**: Monitor. Re-evaluate annually.
**Score below 30**: Single source acceptable. Maintain basic supply market awareness.

Run this scoring for every sole-source component with more than $25,000 annual spend. The 5–10 components that score 80+ are your highest-priority dual-source investments — and almost every manufacturer we have worked with over 35 years has had at least 5 that fall into this category.
