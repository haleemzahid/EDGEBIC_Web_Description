## What is Supply Chain Management?

Supply chain management (SCM) is the active design, planning, execution, control, and monitoring of all activities involved in sourcing raw materials, transforming them into finished products, and delivering those products to end customers. SCM seeks to maximize customer value — measured by on-time delivery, product quality, and competitive pricing — while minimizing total supply chain cost across all participants. It is an integrative discipline that coordinates decisions across organizational boundaries, aligning the objectives of suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, and retailers into a coherent strategy.

## How Supply Chain Management Works

SCM operates at three levels: strategic (long-term network design), tactical (medium-term planning), and operational (day-to-day execution).

**Strategic SCM** makes decisions that shape the supply chain for years: which suppliers to partner with, where to locate factories and warehouses, whether to make or buy components, and what technology platforms to invest in. These decisions determine the structure and capability of the supply chain.

**Tactical SCM** makes medium-term decisions that optimize the network: demand forecasting, production planning, inventory policy setting, transportation mode selection, and supplier allocation. These decisions typically span a 3 to 18 month horizon and are reviewed monthly.

**Operational SCM** executes the daily activities: processing customer orders, scheduling production, releasing purchase orders, managing warehouse operations, dispatching shipments, and handling exceptions. This is where the supply chain either delivers on its promises or fails.

Key SCM processes include: **demand management** (understanding what customers will need), **supply planning** (ensuring materials and capacity are available), **production scheduling** (converting plans into actionable work orders), **inventory optimization** (positioning the right stock at the right locations), **logistics management** (moving goods efficiently), and **supplier collaboration** (sharing information and aligning incentives with key suppliers).

The foundation of effective SCM is information sharing. When supply chain partners share demand forecasts, inventory levels, production schedules, and shipment status in real time, the entire chain becomes more responsive and less reliant on buffer inventories. Technology platforms — ERP systems, supply chain planning tools, and EDI networks — enable this information flow.

## Supply Chain Management Example

A manufacturer of industrial valves implements an integrated SCM approach. Previously, procurement, production, and distribution operated as independent silos — each optimizing its own metrics without coordination.

Procurement ordered materials in large batches to secure volume discounts, resulting in 12 weeks of raw material inventory. Production scheduled large runs to minimize setups, building 8 weeks of finished goods. Distribution maintained safety stock at each of 6 warehouses, adding another 4 weeks of inventory. Total pipeline inventory: 24 weeks of supply, or $8.4 million.

Under integrated SCM: procurement shifts to blanket contracts with weekly releases, reducing raw material inventory to 4 weeks. Production implements finite capacity scheduling with smaller, more frequent runs, reducing finished goods buildup to 3 weeks. Distribution uses DRP to coordinate replenishment across warehouses, reducing distribution inventory to 2 weeks. Total pipeline inventory: 9 weeks, or $3.15 million — a 63 percent reduction.

On-time delivery improves from 89 to 96 percent because smaller, more frequent production runs align more closely with actual customer demand. The freed working capital of $5.25 million is redeployed into new product development and capacity expansion.

## Why Supply Chain Management Matters for Scheduling

Production scheduling is the operational heartbeat of supply chain management. The schedule translates strategic plans and tactical forecasts into specific actions on the shop floor. SCM provides the scheduler with the inputs needed to create an effective schedule: demand requirements, material availability dates, capacity constraints, and delivery commitments.

Scheduling software like [Resource Manager DB (RMDB)](/resource-manager-db-2) is a critical SCM execution tool. It ensures that production capacity is used efficiently, customer due dates are met, and material consumption aligns with procurement plans. When the scheduling system is integrated with broader SCM processes, the entire supply chain becomes more responsive and efficient.

The best manufacturers treat scheduling not as an isolated shop floor activity but as the central coordination mechanism that connects supply chain planning to supply chain execution.
